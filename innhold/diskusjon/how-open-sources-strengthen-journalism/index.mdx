---
title: "How open sources strengthen journalism"
lead:  "Open sources allow journalists to monitor the big players in society—in a way that’s grounded in facts and data."
date: "2021-11-18"
type: diskusjon
---

<div className="no-hyphens">
  <p>With the term <em>open sources</em>, journalist Ståle Grut means sources that are open and free to everyone.<sup id="inline-ref-1"><a href="#ref-1">[1]</a></sup> Mr. Grut lists a diverse number of open-sources types, including formal sources such as government databases (e.g., <a href="https://www.brreg.no/">Brønnøysundregistrene</a> and <a href="https://einnsyn.no/">eInnsyn</a>), news articles, and academic articles; as well as more informal sources, such as social media.<sup id="inline-ref-2"><a href="#ref-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
  <p>The sources (or data) are “open” since they’re available to everyone—without requiring that the person have a position of power or other such qualifications.</p>
  <p>Open sources are important in the way that they’re democracy-strengthening tools. Open sources provide journalists with the information they need to be able to do their job—to monitor the big players in society, such as the government and corporations.<sup id="inline-ref-3"><a href="#ref-3">[3]</a></sup> “Making use of open data, user-generated content, and other open-source information”<sup id="inline-ref-4"><a href="#ref-4">[4]</a></sup> is an integral part of that monitoring process (known as “the social contract of the press”).<sup id="inline-ref-5"><a href="#ref-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
  <p>The key here is that the data is <em>freely</em> available—it’s not available only to the governor or the CEO. Because if that data were left only to the people who actually did the matters “subject to criticism,” those news would never get out.<sup id="inline-ref-6"><a href="#ref-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
  <p>Moreover, since open sources can provide journalists with so much data, they promote stories grounded in the “scientific method” that author Philip Meyer says is “definitely needed in journalism.”<sup id="inline-ref-7"><a href="#ref-7">[7]</a></sup> Databases such as SSB let the journalist describe the world accurately, with stories and analyses grounded in hard facts.</p>
  <p>Here’s an example: In October, <em>VG</em> published an article about how students are asking Norway’s new government to change the law so that the Norwegian State Educational Loan Fund (Lånekassen) can provide full-time students with 50 percent greater financial support during students’ education.<sup id="inline-ref-8"><a href="#ref-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
  <p>The article features statistics from SSB to highlight the fact that work income is becoming a bigger part of students’ overall income.<sup id="inline-ref-9"><a href="#ref-9">[9]</a></sup> Thus, the statistics highlight how more students feel a need to have a job alongside their full-time studies.</p>
  <p>The statistics aren’t an integral part of the news story—the article is not a <a href="https://www.hope.uzh.ch/scoms/article/view/j.scoms.2018.01.013"><em>data story</em></a>. The statistics are instead used as what Mr. David Cheruiyot and Mr. Raul Ferrer-Conill call “a qualitative measure of journalistic success.”<sup id="inline-ref-10"><a href="#ref-10">[10]</a></sup> They’re used as evidence and as a label of accountability.</p>
  <p>The “evidence” part here is important. Why? Because leaving data freely available to citizens—not only people in power nor only journalists—actually wraps up the social contract Prof. Helle Sjøvaag discusses in her book. How? I’ve already shown how citizens must be informed for the democracy to work, and that the media are an important player in that game. But in fact, allowing citizens to fact-check and control the data that the journalists themselves use is just as important in making the social contract work. Journalists should indeed be watchdogs of government and citizens. But citizens themselves should also be watchdogs of journalists and the government.<sup id="inline-ref-11"><a href="#ref-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
  <p>Put simply, having resources of data freely available to citizens is important because it grounds everyone—government, media, and citizens—to the facts.</p>
